# Inputs

The Strategy stage begins with gathering the information required to understand the business and establish the direction for the transformation initiative.

The quality of the Strategy stage depends directly on the quality of its inputs. Incomplete or inaccurate information often leads to poor architectural decisions, unrealistic expectations, and unnecessary project risk.

Before beginning Strategy activities, architects should ensure that the necessary business, organizational, financial, operational, and technical information is available.

---

## Purpose

The purpose of this document is to identify the information required before starting the Strategy stage.

These inputs provide the context needed to understand the business, define objectives, identify constraints, and establish the foundation for the remaining ENAF lifecycle.

---

# Business Inputs

Business inputs provide the strategic direction for the initiative.

Typical business inputs include:

- Business Vision
- Business Strategy
- Corporate Objectives
- Digital Transformation Strategy
- Business Drivers
- Business Capability Model
- Organizational Structure
- Business Priorities
- Business Constraints
- Existing Business Roadmaps

These inputs explain **why** the initiative exists.

---

# Stakeholder Inputs

Transformation requires collaboration across multiple business and technical teams.

Identify and engage stakeholders early.

Typical stakeholders include:

- Executive Sponsor
- Business Owners
- Enterprise Architects
- Network Architects
- Security Architects
- Cloud Architects
- Infrastructure Teams
- Operations Teams
- Service Management
- Project Management
- Risk and Compliance
- Finance
- Procurement

Understanding stakeholder expectations reduces misunderstandings later in the project.

---

# Financial Inputs

Every architecture initiative operates within financial constraints.

Understanding the financial context helps architects develop realistic and sustainable solutions.

Typical financial inputs include:

- Approved Budget
- Investment Objectives
- Expected Return on Investment (ROI)
- Cost Reduction Targets
- Operational Cost Constraints
- Procurement Strategy
- Funding Model
- Financial Risks

Architecture decisions should always consider long-term operational costs, not only initial implementation costs.

---

# People and Organizational Inputs

Successful transformation depends on people as much as technology.

Architects should understand:

- Organizational Structure
- Roles and Responsibilities
- Skills Assessment
- Resource Availability
- Internal Expertise
- External Partners
- Training Requirements
- Organizational Readiness

Identifying capability gaps early reduces implementation risk.

---

# Operational Inputs

Understanding current operations is essential.

Examples include:

- Existing Operating Model
- Support Model
- Service Management Processes
- Change Management Process
- Incident Management Process
- Operational KPIs
- Existing Governance Model

These inputs help ensure that the future architecture can be successfully operated after deployment.

---

# Technical Inputs

Although detailed technical assessment occurs during the Current State stage, some high-level technical information is useful during Strategy.

Examples include:

- Existing Network Overview
- Existing Data Centers
- Cloud Providers
- WAN Connectivity
- Internet Connectivity
- Security Architecture
- Major Applications
- Business Critical Services
- Technology Standards

This information provides context without requiring a detailed technical assessment.

---

# Governance Inputs

Transformation initiatives should align with existing governance practices.

Examples include:

- Enterprise Architecture Principles
- Technology Standards
- Governance Policies
- Risk Management Framework
- Decision-Making Process
- Architecture Review Process
- Compliance Requirements

These inputs help ensure that strategy aligns with organizational governance.

---

# Compliance Inputs

Many organizations operate under regulatory obligations.

Architects should understand applicable requirements before defining the transformation strategy.

Examples include:

- Industry Regulations
- Security Standards
- Data Protection Requirements
- Internal Policies
- Audit Requirements
- Contractual Obligations
- Geographic Restrictions

Compliance should be considered from the beginning rather than validated after implementation.

---

# Assumptions and Constraints

Every initiative begins with assumptions and limitations.

Examples include:

## Assumptions

- Executive sponsorship will continue.
- Funding will remain available.
- Business priorities will remain stable.
- Required resources will be available.

## Constraints

- Budget limitations
- Delivery timelines
- Existing contracts
- Legacy technologies
- Regulatory obligations
- Resource availability
- Skills shortages

Documenting assumptions and constraints early helps manage expectations throughout the lifecycle.

---

# Input Validation

Before proceeding, architects should confirm that the collected information is:

- Accurate
- Current
- Complete
- Approved where required
- Understood by key stakeholders
- Available to the architecture team

Missing information should be identified and addressed before progressing further.

---

## Summary

The Strategy stage relies on a broad range of business, financial, organizational, operational, governance, compliance, and technical inputs.

Collecting these inputs ensures that architecture decisions are based on a complete understanding of the organization rather than assumptions or isolated technical requirements.

Strong inputs create a strong strategy, and a strong strategy provides the foundation for a successful enterprise transformation.
